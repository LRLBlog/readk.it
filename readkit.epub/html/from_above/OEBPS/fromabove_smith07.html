W. Eugene Smith, United States 1918–1978. Over Tinian, 1944. Gelatin silver print. W. Eugene Smith Archive 82.102.73. © The Heirs of W. Eugene Smith

This photo of the Tinian siege is impressive for the photographic bravado it implies. For Smith to have taken this picture, he would had to have been in a companion plane a little above and ahead of the plane that is seen—and likewise dodging bomb bursts while virtually scraping the ground. I am not sure today’s war photographers would accept a similar assignment. —Craig Allen, Journalism Professor

Virtually every aircraft used during World War II was capable of carrying a camera to provide images for evaluating the effects of an air strike. As a side effect these official cameras and the thousands of unofficial ones carried by soldiers and airmen gave us a massive body of historical documentation of the largest war in human history. —James Stemm, Curator of Collections, Pima Air & Space Museum

Smith’s image is a classic piece of journalism. It frames smoke, fields, coast and plane in a way that tells me a story. This picture can stand alone, or add a valuable visual element to an article about the battle for Tinian Island. Having shot pictures out of a plane myself, I marvel at the way Smith was able to capture this image in the precise manner he did. “Easier said than done” is an extreme understatement here. —Morgan Loew, Investigative Reporter, CBS 5 News, Phoenix
